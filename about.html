About

Building robotics that can leave the prototype stage

Bir Bikram Dey is a robotics and hardware engineer focused on autonomous systems that have to operate in real environments, not just controlled demos. His work spans mechanical design, embedded control, sensor integration, and field deployment.

Across surface vessels, amphibious platforms, and spherical robots, the through-line is practical engineering: build the platform, test it outside, learn quickly, and refine it until it is robust enough to keep working.

What I Work On

The portfolio centers on autonomous and semi-autonomous robotic systems where sensing, actuation, and mechanical packaging all have to work together. That includes hull and chassis design, onboard electronics, firmware and ROS-based integration, and the practical realities of assembly and testing.

The projects on this site show a consistent interest in systems that move through difficult terrain or constrained environments: shallow water, underwater spaces, hazardous inspection areas, and rolling-body robotic architectures.

How I Build

  • System-first design Mechanical, electrical, and controls decisions are made together so the platform stays balanced and serviceable.
  • Fast prototyping loops CAD, fabrication, bench testing, and field validation are treated as one continuous workflow.
  • Deployment mindset The goal is dependable behavior in real settings, with attention to integration, durability, and recovery.

Core Toolchain

SolidWorks and mechanical packaging for enclosure, hull, and structural design.

Eagle CAD and embedded electronics workflows for control, sensing, and integration.

Visual Studio, firmware development, and robotics software pipelines for real hardware.

Prototype fabrication, instrumentation, and iterative test workflows for field-ready builds.

Project Themes

Autonomous marine monitoring Varuna and MARE show work on surface robotics, exploration, and environmental observation. Amphibious and underwater mobility AQUA explores locomotion strategies beyond traditional propeller-only robotic movement. Protected robotic architectures DragonBall and Spherico investigate spherical formats for resilience, control, and low-cost experimentation.
